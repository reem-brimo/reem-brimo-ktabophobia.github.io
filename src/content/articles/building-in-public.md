There's something terrifying about showing unfinished work.

Every maker knows this feeling. You have an idea, you start building, and there's a period where anything could happen. The project could become something meaningful, or it could crash and burn spectacularly. In this phase, everything is uncertain, fragile, and deeply personal.

Most of us hide during this phase. We wait until the work is polished, presentable, ready for consumption. We share only our victories, never the messy process that preceded them.

But what if we didn't?

## The Vulnerability of Early Work

Last year, I started sharing projects while they were still rough. The first commit, the ugly prototype, the debugging session that went nowhere. I expected rejection. What I got instead was something unexpected: connection.

When people see your process, they see themselves in it. The struggles you face aren't unique failures — they're universal experiences. That bug you spent three hours on? Someone else fought the same battle last week. That feature you're unsure about? Someone has exactly the feedback you need.

## The Unexpected Benefits

**Accountability** — When people are watching, you're less likely to abandon projects mid-way. The social commitment creates a gentle pressure to finish.

**Faster iteration** — Early feedback means course-correcting before you've gone too far down the wrong path. The cost of change is highest in late stages.

**Authentic audience** — People who follow your process aren't casual consumers. They're invested. They care about the project and your growth as a maker.

**Documentation** — Your struggle becomes someone else's resource. The answer you figured out after hours of frustration? That blog post writes itself.

## The Fear Is Overstated

I worried about looking incompetent. What if someone saw me stuck on something obvious? What if my code was terrible?

The reality: most people are dealing with their own struggles. They're not judging you. They're relieved to see someone else fighting the same battles.

The work that looks effortless is rarely created effortlessly. Building in public demystifies the process and reminds everyone — experts included — that mastery is a journey, not a destination.

---

The next time you're building something, consider opening a window. Share the uncertainty. Document the process. You might find that the most valuable thing you create isn't the product itself, but the story of how you built it.
