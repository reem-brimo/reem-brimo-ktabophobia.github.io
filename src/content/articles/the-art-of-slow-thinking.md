There's a particular pressure that comes with modern knowledge work. We celebrate quick thinkers, rapid responders, those who can fire off answers before the question is fully formed. Being fast has become synonymous with being smart.

But I've been questioning this lately.

## The Speed Trap

Early in my career, I prided myself on being quick. I could respond to emails instantly, make decisions on the spot, and always had an opinion ready. It felt productive. It felt like competence.

What I didn't realize was that speed was becoming a substitute for depth. I was racing through problems, finding surface-level solutions, and moving on before anything had time to settle.

> "The successful warrior is the average man, with laser-like focus." — Bruce Lee

This captures something important. True focus isn't about speed — it's about intensity and direction.

## What Slow Thinking Looks Like

Slow thinking isn't about being lazy or indecisive. It's about creating space for ideas to develop naturally.

When I encounter a complex problem now, I try to resist the urge to solve it immediately. Instead, I let it incubate, write without editing first, and return to old problems with fresh eyes.

## Making Space for Depth

I've found practices that help: morning pages, dedicated focus time, walking meetings, and weekly reviews.

The irony is that slow thinking often produces faster results in the long run. Solutions that emerge from deep consideration tend to be more robust and more likely to actually solve the problem at hand.

Next time you feel the urge to quickly resolve something, consider the alternative. Give it time. The best thinking often happens when we stop trying to think faster.
